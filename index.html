The Abstract Woman

Hilma af Klint

The first Western abstract paintings known were done by Swedish woman Hilma af Klint (born 1862). Klint spent many summers at her family manor on the island of Adelso in Lake Malaren, where she became surrounded and found herself profoundly in contact with nature. Klint was a part of a group, “The Five” which consisted of four other women who were highly inspired by Theosophy. Klint’s paintings tend to be visual representations of highly complex spiritual concepts. The Five were known to believe in reaching out to higher powers typically in the form of seances. Klint’s abstraction predates the very men whose names are the definition of abstraction within the art community; Kandinsky, Mondrian, Malevich and Kupka.

Early Life

Hilma was the fourth child in her family. Her Parents were Mathilda af Klint and Captain Victor af Klint of the Swedish navy. Hilma’s family was the source of her interest in the fields of mathematics and botany. She demonstrated a considerable ability in visual art at a young age, and began studying at an art school in Stockholm after her family moved there. It was here that she learned portraiture and landscape painting.

At the age of twenty she was admitted at the Royal Academy of Fine Arts. Here she studied drawing, portraiture, and landscape painting. She excelled at the academy, graduating with honors and being given her own studio as a scholarship. The building housing the studio was owned by the Royal Academy of Fine Arts, and was the main cultural hub in Stockholm at the time. It also housed Blanch’s Cafe and Blanch’s Art Gallery, which was a site of conflict between conventional artistic views and the more contemporary views of the “Art Society” inspired by French En Plein Air painters. After graduating, Hilma began working in Stockholm and gaining recognition for her artwork.

Work

While at the Academy of fine arts, Klint met a women named Anna Cassel, who was the first of the other four women who would later form “The Five”. The Five began as members of the Edelweiss Society, which embraced theosophical teachings of Helena Blavatsy as well as spiritualism. The Five also had an interest in the paranormal and frequently, held seances. Every one of their meetings began with a prayer, meditation, a Christian sermon, and an analysis of a text from the New Testament, then closing with a seance. The group created and recorded a new system of mystical thought which was inspired by messages from higher spirits they called The High Masters. In essence, the system upheld spiritual knowledge above intellect.

Klint’s work with The Five led to her experimentation with automatic drawing as early as 1896, which further developed into her inventive geometric visual language. With this she explored religion, atoms, and nature, and she documented her findings extensively. Supposedly, she was tasked by the High Masters to create the paintings for the so-called “Temple”, but she never fully understood what this meant. She felt she was being guided by a force which would physically influence the movement of her hand.

In 1906, after a 20 year artistic career, Klint painted her first series of abstract paintings. She also completed the works for the Temple between 1906 and 1915, done in two phases with a hiatus from 1908 to 1912. As she developed her new abstract style, she once again developed a new artistic language. The spiritual remained the main source of creativity for the remainder of her life.

Legacy

Hilma Klint specified in her will that her work should be kept a secret for at least 20 years after her death. She did this because she felt her work was too advanced for people at the time. When the boxes of her work were eventually opened, only a small group of people had knowledge of what was going to be revealed.

Her art was introduced to an international audience in 1984 thanks to the art historian Ake Fant, when he presented it at a Nordik conference in Helsinki. Her collection of abstract work includes more than 1200 works, and is now managed by a foundation named for her and located in Stockholm.
